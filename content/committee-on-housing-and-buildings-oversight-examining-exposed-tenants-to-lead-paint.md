Summary

Meeting Overview

The Housing and Buildings Committee held an oversight hearing on preserving affordable housing in distress and reforming the Third-Party Transfer Program. Chair Sanchez opened by contextualizing NYC's foreclosure powers, dating back to the 1970s fiscal crisis when the city lost 350,000 housing units to abandonment. The Third-Party Transfer Program, created in 1996 as a more targeted alternative to direct city ownership, allows foreclosure on tax-delinquent distressed properties with transfer to qualified developers rather than city control. However, TPT has been suspended since 2019 following criticism of Round 10's administration, which swept up non-distressed properties and disproportionately affected communities of color.

HPD testified in support of Intro 657, the "Safer Homes Act," which would overhaul TPT using a "critical eligible" model targeting the worst buildings through an indexing system combining municipal debt and housing violations. Key reforms include eliminating the problematic "block pickup" provision, requiring more stringent tenant notification, and creating pathways for resident ownership including HDFC cooperative reconstitution. HPD's analysis suggests this approach would capture larger, more distressed buildings (averaging 30 units versus 10 under current law) with more geographically diverse distribution.

Significant tensions emerged around tenant ownership guarantees and agency discretion. Council Members Epstein and Sanchez pressed HPD on whether tenants who complete all cooperative training requirements would be guaranteed building ownership, noting past instances where buildings remained in limbo for decades. HPD maintained they would provide clear timelines and milestones but resisted statutory guarantees, preferring programmatic flexibility. The discussion revealed that of 73 tenant petition properties from previous rounds, only 47 successfully converted to cooperatives, with 13 remaining long-term rentals and others still pending conversion years later.

The hearing also addressed concerns about HDFC cooperatives, which comprised roughly 20% of the hypothetical top 500 distressed buildings under the new model. Council members questioned why cooperatives that never received adequate city support during initial conversion might face foreclosure again, and pressed for stronger protections and technical assistance. HPD proposed enhanced outreach, standardized payment plans, and an owner resource center, while acknowledging the need for surplus equity provisions following recent Supreme Court decisions.
